Slope is defined between two points. So how can a curve have a steepness at a single one?

You know only one point on this curve — the purple dot. Your usual steepness tool needs two points, so you can't use it. But you can lay a straight stick across your point and tilt it. Let's see what happens.

  1. Drag the teal handle to tilt the orange stick around your point.
  2. Tilt until the stick skims along the curve — pointing the way the curve goes right at your dot, touching but not slicing through.
  3. Watch the hollow dot: it's the other place your stick cuts the curve. As you skim, see where it goes.

You found it — now here's the real puzzle.

There is exactly one straight stick that skims the curve right at your point. Lay any other stick and it slices clean through; only this one rests along the curve, touching at a single place — yours. That skimming stick is how steep the curve is at that exact point.

But a stick is a straight line — and to measure how steep a straight line is, you have always needed two points on it. Here you know only one: the place where it just touches. So how could we ever find the steepness of this stick, knowing only that single touching point?

One clue you just saw: the moment your stick began to skim, the other crossing point slid in and vanished into your point — two points quietly became one. Hold on to that. It is the key to the whole mystery.
